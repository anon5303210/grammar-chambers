# Corpus report — Grant batch 2 (2023–2024 opinions)

Read in full: *Nehme v. FIU* (2024-12-02, ADA), *Moms for Liberty v. Brevard* (2024-10-08,
First Amendment, ~26pp), *McCreight v. AuburnBank* (2024-09-19, Title VII/ADEA, ~33pp),
*Ctr. for a Sustainable Coast v. Army Corps* (2024-05-02, NEPA standing), *Honeyfund.com v.
Governor* (2024-03-04, Stop WOKE Act), *United States v. Pate* (2023-10-11, her en banc
DISSENT, ~11pp). *Georgia v. Clark* was per curiam with only a one-sentence Grant
concurrence — excluded. Effective corpus: 5 majorities + 1 dissent, ~116 pages.

## Key findings (converging with batch 1)
1. **Commas**: serial always; that/which textbook (restrictive *which* only inside quotes);
   introductory comma nearly always even short ("In fact," "To start,") with occasional
   single-word-adverb exceptions — VARIES. Interpolated attributions mid-sentence are a
   signature: "The dock, it said, had already been built"; "their fear, the Board says, is
   not objectively reasonable." Comma before conjunction in compound disposition sentences
   sometimes omitted — VARIES.
2. **Semicolons** fuse tight clauses ("Maybe NEPA review would result in denial; maybe
   not.") and separate long list elements after colons. **Colons** frequent before
   explanations and block quotes ("fails for a basic reason: ..."; "The bottom line is
   this: ..."). **Em dashes**: signature mark, unspaced, up to three in one sentence;
   paired-dash interpolations ("Moms for Liberty—not to mention the individual
   plaintiffs—cannot"). Parentheses for small asides ("(and should)", "(its proponents say)").
3. **Architecture**: short declaratives/fragments ("He did." "Not so." "True." "No such
   luck." "Or principal. Or coach. And so on."); constant And/But/So/Nor openers; Nor with
   inversion ("Nor can we forget Wollschlaeger"); one-word rhetorical questions ("Why?"
   "Also missing? Any explanation..."); narrative hooks ("Elie Nehme did not do well in
   medical school.").
4. **Agreement**: institutions singular + it/its. Usually clean neither/nor singular
   ("neither woman offered"; "and neither will we"). LIVE SLIPS in the corpus (good
   proofreading drill material): "neither of its claims were redressable"; "only forcible
   assault or intimidation qualify"; "none of these mistakes were severe" (notional —
   accepted); subjunctive was/were inconsistent — VARIES.
5. **Pronouns**: generic *she* is default for students/plaintiffs/employees; *he* persists
   for officers/defendants; mixes he/she across adjacent sentences (Pate). Singular they
   attested once ("the message they wish to convey"). Repeats surnames in multi-actor
   paragraphs; "who to silence" (not whom).
6. **Capitalization**: this Court/our Court/this Circuit capped; district court lowercase;
   defined party short forms capped ("the Board," "the Center") BUT "the state" lowercase
   for Florida-as-party; *Nehme* flips University/university — VARIES. "Covid-19";
   Constitution capped, constitutional not.
7. **Hyphenation**: attributive hyphenated, predicate open ("viewpoint-based
   discrimination" / "was viewpoint based") with real exceptions (conclusion of Moms for
   Liberty hyphenates predicate "viewpoint-neutral") — pattern, not absolute. **Suspended
   hyphens used often**: "content- and viewpoint-based"; "single- or mixed-motive."
   Closed: decisionmaker, decisionmaking, caselaw, nonmoving, postsecondary. Quirks:
   "an as applied challenge" unhyphenated; "over relying"; spelling "imbedded."
8. **Possessives**: "Congress's," "the Corps's"; joint possession on last name only
   ("McCreight and Wester's ADEA claims"); "attorney's fees."
9. **Contractions**: a few per opinion at conversational beats ("That's when clinical
   clerkships began"; "we'll admit"; "we can't know"), never in holdings.
10. **Numbers**: words well past ten ("fifty percent extra time," "over sixty years old,"
    "ninety-day"); numerals for dollars, measurements, statutes, dates ("$100,000,"
    "500-square-foot dock," "20,500 acres").
11. **Tense**: past facts; present for texts ("Title VII prohibits"); present perfect for
    precedent as a body ("We have said"), simple past for a specific decision ("In Berry,
    we rejected").
12. **Parallelism**: inline (1)(2)(3) — commas when short, semicolons when long, always
    conjunction before last; indented numbered block quotes for statutory lists; rhetorical
    parallelism ("Banning speech on... topics is bad; banning speech on... viewpoints is
    worse.").
13. **Habits**: "Respectfully, ..." as polite rebuke (3 opinions); "To be sure, ... But ..."
    everywhere; "(quotation omitted)" family, never "(cleaned up)"; "* * *" before coda;
    ALL-CAPS dispositions; dissents end "I respectfully dissent."; vivid idiom ("all roads
    lead to Rule 56"; "the sentence buckles under the weight Florida asks it to bear");
    first-person singular slips into majority footnotes ("I will not belabor...").
14. **Proofreading-risk**: multi-dash sentences; suspended hyphens (dropping the hanging
    hyphen); attributive/predicate hyphen flips; list punctuation consistency; neither/nor
    and or-compound agreement; "it" antecedents with two institutions; joint + s-ending
    possessives; nested quotes; closed compounds vs. spell-checker defaults; he/she
    consistency; subjunctive.

## Variance flags (never binary drills)
University/university; predicate hyphenation; "as applied challenge"; comma after
single-word adverbs; subjunctive was/were; singular they; comma before conjunction in
dispositions; "over relying"/"imbedded."
