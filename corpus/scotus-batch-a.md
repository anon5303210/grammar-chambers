# Corpus report — SCOTUS batch A (Roberts, Kagan, Barrett; 2024–2025)

Six majority opinions read in full (syllabi/concurrences/dissents excluded): *Loper Bright
v. Raimondo* (Roberts, 2024), *United States v. Skrmetti* (Roberts, 2025), *Smith & Wesson
v. Estados Unidos Mexicanos* (Kagan, 2025), *FCC v. Consumers' Research* (Kagan, 2025),
*Trump v. CASA* (Barrett, 2025), *Medical Marijuana v. Horn* (Barrett, 2025).

## Shared, testable core (agrees with Grant corpus)
- Serial comma universal. That/which discipline strict (restrictive *that*, comma + *which*).
- Introductory-element commas nearly always ("In 2012, ..."; "Months later, ...").
- **Capital after colon when a full sentence follows — all three writers, consistently**
  ("The answer is no: Neither the universal injunction nor..."); lowercase for fragments/lists.
- Em dash the workhorse interrupter ("The Government—unsurprisingly—sees matters
  differently."). Full-sentence parentheticals carry terminal punctuation inside.
- Sentence-initial And/But/So/Nor/Yet/Or standard. Short declarative pivots ("We disagree."
  "Not so." "Chevron is overruled."). Deliberate fragments as rhetoric ("Indeed."; "The
  bottom line?").
- Institutional singulars ("Congress has"; "the Court holds"; "Tennessee determined");
  none + singular ("none of these considerations justifies"); neither/nor + singular
  ("Neither the universal injunction nor a sufficiently comparable predecessor was
  available"); "a substantial majority of Americans has access."
- Antecedent discipline = noun repetition over pronoun chains ("the Commission"/"the
  Administrator" re-named for pages).
- Capitalization: "this Court" (SCOTUS); "the District Court"/"the Second Circuit" capped
  as proper names, lowercase generic re-reference; State capped as sovereign, lowercase
  adjectivally; "the Government" (federal litigant), "Executive Branch," "the Framers,"
  "the Nation."
- Hyphenation: compound modifier hyphenated before noun, open after — Barrett states it by
  example: "hard to make a term-of-art argument without the term of art." Suspended
  hyphens ("18th- and 19th-century"; "age- and diagnosis-based").
- Possessives: modern default 's after s-final singulars ("Congress's," "Direct Sales's");
  older QUOTED material keeps "Congress'" verbatim — the two conventions legitimately
  coexist, split by quotation boundaries; never conform inside quotes.
- Numbers: spell one–nine, numerals 10+ ("14 days," "127 universal injunctions");
  sentence-initial numbers spelled ("Ninety-six of them"); both terms of a comparison as
  numerals even below ten ("2 to 4").
- Tense: present for statutes ("Section 1964(c) provides"); past for procedure; precedent
  past when narrating ("we held"), present for its continuing rule ("Sedima holds");
  present perfect for accumulated doctrine.
- Parallelism: verb-form parity in numbered lists; antithesis-as-parallel ("It prevents
  them from judging.").

## Writer variation (never binary drills)
Contractions (Roberts zero; Barrett rare-deliberate "You can't replace something with
nothing."; Kagan none in these majorities); generic pronoun he (Kagan) vs. she (Barrett)
vs. singular they once (Roberts, tracking Title VII); "rational basis review" open
(Roberts) vs. hyphenated (Thomas); % symbol (Kagan) vs. "percent" (Roberts); spelled vs.
numeral decades; rhetorical-question density; Thomas's "But, ..." comma-after-conjunction
habit (none of these three).

## REAL ERRORS found in slip opinions (gold for find-the-flaw drills)
1. "it is **unecessary** to consider" (CASA majority) — typo in a published SCOTUS opinion.
2. "Medical Marijuana, together with the principal dissent, **warn** that..." (Horn) —
   "together with" doesn't pluralize; should be *warns*.
3. "founded on a **third-party's** criminal use" (Smith & Wesson) — hyphenated noun
   possessive; the same opinion writes open noun "a third party."
4. "a qualifying 'legal right?'" (Horn) — sentence-level question mark placed inside
   quotes; convention puts it outside.
5. CASA n.11: "Zamecnik v. **Indiana** Prairie School Dist." — case is *Indian Prairie*;
   citation-name fidelity is a high-risk zone.
6. "Tr. of Oral Arg 80" — missing period after "Arg."
7. "surely they would not have let it **lay** idle" (CASA) — lay/lie slip in a majority.
8. Quotes preserving archaic conventions ("coöperation," "Congress'") — a proofreader must
   never "correct" inside quotation marks.
9. Long conditional chains with buried agreement ("If, says the statute, a substantial
   majority of Americans has access to a communications service that is both affordable
   and essential..., then...").
