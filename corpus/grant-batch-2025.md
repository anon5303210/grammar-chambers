# Corpus report — Grant batch 3 (2025 + Jan 2026 opinions)

All seven read in full (~180 pages of Grant prose): *Finney v. MetLife* (2026-01-26, FEGLI
contract), *United States v. Keegan* (2025-12-17, FRE 803(4)), *Johnson v. Reliance
Standard* (2025-11-21, ERISA), *Oakes Farms v. Adkins* (2025-09-17, First Amendment
retaliation), *Dershowitz v. CNN* (2025-08-29, defamation), *Burt v. President of UF*
(2025-08-20, appellate jurisdiction), *Otto Candies v. Citigroup* (2025-05-08, RICO, 82pp).
She authored all seven majorities; others' concurrences/dissents excluded.

## Key findings
1. **Commas**: serial always; that/which strictly disciplined (no restrictive *which* in
   ~180 pages; quotes left intact); introductory comma nearly always, though sentence-
   initial "So" often runs in. Appositive names in commas ("The bank's then-CEO, Michael
   Corbat, explained"); glosses in em dashes ("an endocrinologist—a doctor specializing in
   hormones—"). Deliberate asyndetic correlative: "not only de novo wrong, it is also
   unreasonable."
2. **Em dash = signature mark**, several per page, always unspaced: definition, emphasis
   ("disagreed—strongly—"), aside ("the final—and only—decisionmaker"), pivot. One en dash
   in "then–CNN President Jeff Zucker." **Colons** heavily used ("boils down to one
   question: ..."; fragment openers "An example:"; "The gist:"; "Simply put:").
   **Question-fragment device**: "The result? A cycle in which..."; "The problem? ..."
   Parentheses wry: "(yes, him again)"; "it could (would) deny benefits."
3. **Architecture**: fragments ("Denied." "None." "That's it." "True enough. But also
   beside the point."); And/But/So/Yet/Nor/Or openers constantly; self-answered rhetorical
   questions; imperative transitions ("Take the business records exception." "Start with
   the December 2013..."); narrative openers ("Cheriese Johnson was not feeling well.");
   inverted conditionals ("Had Rabobank known the truth...").
4. **Agreement**: institutions uniformly singular + its; "none grapples" (singular here —
   notional agreement varies across corpus); "Neither is persuasive"; "Neither Johnson nor
   any of her many doctors... ever suspected."
5. **Pronouns**: repeats nouns/role-nouns ("the bank," "the insurer," "the network") over
   pronouns; generic individuals gendered, mixed by context; NO singular they in this batch.
6. **Capitalization**: this Court/this Circuit capped; district court lowercase; "the Rule,"
   "the Note" capped for specific rules; "our Nation's"; parties lowercase generically but
   capped as titles ("Plaintiff Otto Candies"); "the University" capped as party shorthand;
   Covid-19 in her prose (COVID-19 preserved in quotes); "preexisting" normalized in her
   prose vs. "Pre-Existing" in quoted policy. Disposition lowercase in intro ("we affirm"),
   ALL CAPS at the end ("we AFFIRM").
7. **Hyphenation**: aggressive attributive hyphenation, open as predicate ("cash-advance
   facility"/"cash advances"; "wire-fraud allegations"/"wire fraud"; "arbitrary-and-
   capricious review"); "then-CEO," "motion-to-dismiss stage," "prior-panel-precedent
   rule," "garden-variety errors." Closed: decisionmaker, factfinder, lookback, email,
   groupthink; verb "to end-run." VARIES: "common-law fraud" vs. "common law aiding and
   abetting fraud" in the same list (Otto Candies p.11) — never binary-drill hyphen
   variances.
8. **Possessives**: singular s-names take 's ("Reynolds's," "Oakes's," "witness's,"
   "Schuurman's") BUT **plural-form entity names take bare apostrophe: "Oakes Farms' own
   practices"** — adjacent to "Oakes's," a high-value drill contrast.
9. **Contractions**: selective — that's/it's/what's/there's/we've freely; **never contracts
   negatives or modals** ("does not," "cannot" always). One sentence shows the split: "And
   that's true even though Burt did not amend."
10. **Numbers**: spelled through one hundred ("sixty-seven years old," "twenty-seven days,"
    "two hundred years ago"); numerals for money, percentages ("almost 600%"), data-dense
    counts ("166 cash advance requests," "nearly 3,000 comments"), dates. **Date-as-
    adjective keeps both commas: "a March 20, 2012, email" — strong recurring habit.**
11. **Tense**: past facts; present for texts ("Rule 803(4) provides"); past for prior
    courts' actions, present for a precedent's content ("Ferrizzi is entirely consistent");
    present perfect for cross-reference ("As we have explained"). Argument verbs varied:
    argues, contends, insists, protests, counters, urges.
12. **Parallelism**: inline "(1)...; (2)...; and (3)..." with quoted elements; **bullet
    lists (•) used liberally** (unusual for judicial prose); prepositional anaphora
    ("Between the plain text..., the background..., and the district court's findings...").
13. **Habits**: textualist opener formula "We start, as always, with the text..." (3 of 7);
    intro ends "Because X, we affirm/reverse"; "* * *" + aphoristic coda + ALL-CAPS
    disposition; "To be sure, ... But ..." in nearly every opinion; idioms: "move the
    needle," "holds water," "nonstarter," "full stop," "First up," "Worse yet," "All told,"
    "smoking gun," "the fox... guarding the henhouse"; "(quotation omitted)" — never
    "(cleaned up)," never "(internal quotation marks omitted)"; short standalone
    rejections ("We disagree." "We see things differently."); sparing italic Latin.
14. **Proofreading-risk**: em-dash interpolations splitting subject/verb ("Neither Johnson
    nor any of her many doctors—a neurologist, pulmonologist, thoracic surgeon, and
    rheumatologist—ever suspected"); date double commas; phrasal-adjective hyphen flips;
    Oakes's vs. Oakes Farms'; its/it's density; bracketed alterations ("[s]taff,"
    "believe[d]"); numbered-series punctuation; quoted-source fidelity vs. her
    normalization (Covid/COVID, preexisting/Pre-Existing, lookback/look-back — a clerk
    "harmonizing" either direction errs); deliberate fragments; inverted conditionals;
    who/whom in stacked clauses ("commentators who Dershowitz says defamed him" —
    correct *who*; "Dershowitz, who no one disputes is a public figure").

## Variance flags (never binary drills)
common-law premodifier hyphenation; "In short,"/"Simply put," comma vs. colon; generic
he/she; heading formats; numeral boundary in data-dense passages; publication-slug
formatting.
