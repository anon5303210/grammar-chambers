# Corpus report — Grant batch 1 (2026 opinions)

Six Grant-authored majorities read in full (~155 pages): *Akerlund v. Atlas Air* (2026-07-10;
note: search listed this cluster as "Estate of Lane Caviness v. Atlas Air" but the fetched
document is captioned Akerlund, No. 24-11033 — same court/date/author), *Pernell v. Fla. Bd.
of Governors* (2026-07-07, ~50pp Grant portion), *Jackson v. Jones* (2026-06-12), *Mobile
Baykeeper v. Alabama Power* (2026-05-18), *United States v. Blair* (2026-05-01), *Foster v.
King* (2026-03-18). *Davis v. Comm'r Ala. DOC* (en banc denial) contained no Grant-authored
text (she joined Branch's concurrence) — excluded from style data.

## Findings

1. **Commas**: Serial comma always. That/which discipline strict — restrictive = *that*;
   *which* always nonrestrictive with comma; "of which" chains replace "whose." Introductory
   comma VARIES for single-word adverbs ("Currently, ..." but "Ultimately the district
   court..."). Sentence-initial And/But/So/Nor/Yet take no comma. No comma splices.
   Nonrestrictive appositives with commas; restrictive close appositives without ("the
   plaintiffs' counsel Anthony F. Sabatini").
2. **Semicolons/colons/dashes/parens**: Semicolons for internally punctuated numbered lists
   "(1) ...; (2) ...; and (3) ..." and occasionally tight independent clauses. Colon before
   explanation/punchline is a signature move ("resulting in a new rule: ..."). Em dashes very
   frequent, always unspaced; "—not X" contrast dash recurring; paired dashes for long
   interruptions. Parentheses for witty asides.
3. **Sentence architecture**: medium sentences + very short declaratives and deliberate
   fragments ("No." "Not so." "Check." "Even after being warned."). Freely opens with
   And/But/So/Nor/Plus/Yet. Rhetorical questions, sometimes stacked (Injury? ... Check.).
   One-sentence closer paragraphs. Narrative/aphoristic opening hooks.
4. **Agreement**: collective/corporate entities singular ("Atlas Air is"; "the government
   argues") then it/its. "Neither general nor specific jurisdiction is available." Notional
   "None are persuasive" (don't drill "none is" as only correct). "EPA" without article.
5. **Pronouns**: elegant variation over pronouns for multi-actor clarity (Alabama Power →
   "the utility," "the company"); repeats names in criminal facts. Generic he and she both
   used; singular they appears rarely ("anyone lost their job") — VARIES, not drillable.
6. **Capitalization**: this Court/the Supreme Court capped; district/trial court lowercase.
   State capped as sovereign party, lowercase adjectivally ("state action"). Parties
   lowercase ("the plaintiffs"). Offices capped when specific ("Governor"). "Covid-19";
   "our Nation's" capped.
7. **Hyphenation**: heavy premodifier hyphenation ("pandemic-era policies," "case-by-case
   basis," "motion-to-dismiss stage," "salary-for-speech rule"); -ly adverb compounds never
   hyphenated; open after the noun ("well underway"); closed compounds in her own prose:
   **decisionmaking**, noncompliant, nonparties, postsecondary, anticorruption, policymaking
   (quoted material may differ — variance flag).
8. **Possessives**: singular s-names take 's — "Jones's," "Arias's," "witness's." Plural
   standard ("the plaintiffs' counsel"). "twenty years' imprisonment." Double genitive
   ("a friend of Arias's").
9. **Contractions**: used but rationed — conversational beats inside formal prose ("That's
   no surprise."; "doesn't get much more straightforward"); formal analysis defaults to
   full forms. A handful per opinion.
10. **Numbers**: words through ninety-nine ("ninety-four travelers," "eighty-four months");
    numerals 100+ and money ("$434,195," "over $73 million"); "percent" as a word
    ("fifteen percent"); en dash year ranges ("2008–2009").
11. **Tense**: past for history/conduct; present for what texts say ("Rule 602 requires");
    precedent: past for what a court did ("we affirmed"), present-perfect for what it stands
    for ("this Court has").
12. **Parallelism**: inline (1)/(2)/(3) semicolon lists with "; and" penultimate; indented
    numbered lists for long element sets; First/Second/Third signposting; echo parallelism
    ("no liquids in—and no liquids out").
13. **Distinctive habits**: never "(cleaned up)" — uses "(quotation omitted)," "(alteration
    adopted and quotation omitted)" etc., singular/plural per instance. Centered "* * *"
    before summations. Disposition verbs in caps ("We AFFIRM"). Recurring phrases: "The sum
    of it is," "So too here," "What's more," "At bottom," "misses the mark," "does not move
    the needle," "falls short." Wit and idiom core to voice. Per-issue standards of review
    sometimes footnoted (*Blair*).
14. **Proofreading-risk spots**: subject–verb agreement across paired-em-dash interruptions;
    "of which" chains; neither/nor + singular verb; s-possessives (don't "fix" Jones's to
    Jones'); bracketed alterations in quotes ("impair[ing]"); deliberate fragments vs.
    accidental; parallelism across inline numbered lists; Court/court and State/state by
    context; closed compounds (don't insert hyphens); number-style boundary at one hundred;
    "quotation" vs. "quotations" in parentheticals; notional agreement.

## Variance flags (never binary drills)
Comma after single-word introductory adverbs; decisionmaking vs. decision-making (quoted);
generic he/she/rare singular they; contraction frequency; standards of review in text vs.
footnotes.
