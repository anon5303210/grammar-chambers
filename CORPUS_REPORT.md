## The consistent core (drillable as right/wrong)

These practices are uniform across Grant's ~450 pages AND the SCOTUS sample:

1. **Serial comma, always.**
2. **That/which discipline**: restrictive = *that* (no comma); *which* only after a comma
   (or preposition). Zero restrictive *which* in Grant's own prose across three batches.
3. **No comma splices.** Semicolons join related independent clauses; semicolons separate
   internally punctuated list items with "; and" before the last.
4. **Institutional singulars**: courts, agencies, companies, "the government" take
   singular verbs and *it/its*.
5. **Neither/nor with singular elements takes a singular verb** ("neither general nor
   specific jurisdiction is available") — and the corpus contains live slips of this rule
   in published opinions, making it prime drill material.
6. **Agreement survives interrupters**: subject–verb agreement across em-dash asides,
   "together with" phrases, and long prepositional chains (a real SCOTUS slip: "Medical
   Marijuana, together with the principal dissent, warn" — should be *warns*).
7. **Possessives**: singular nouns ending in s take **'s** (Congress's, Jones's, Corps's,
   witness's); plural and plural-form names take bare apostrophe (plaintiffs', Oakes
   Farms' — adjacent to "Oakes's" in the same opinion); joint possession marks the last
   name only (McCreight and Wester's claims).
8. **Hyphenation**: compound modifier hyphenated before the noun, open after it
   ("cash-advance facility" / "cash advances"); suspended hyphens ("content- and
   viewpoint-based"); -ly adverb compounds never hyphenated.
9. **Em dashes unspaced**, paired dashes must close; punchline colons; capital after a
   colon when a full sentence follows (uniform at SCOTUS; Grant compatible).
10. **Quotation punctuation**: periods and commas inside closing quotes; semicolons and
    colons outside; question marks by sense. **Never "correct" anything inside quotation
    marks** — quoted archaic forms (Congress', coöperation, COVID-19) stay verbatim even
    when the writer's own prose differs (Congress's, Covid-19).
11. **Numbers**: Grant spells through one hundred and writes "percent"; numerals for money,
    dates, statutes, and data-dense counts; sentence-initial numbers always spelled. The
    date-as-adjective keeps both commas: "a March 20, 2012, email."
12. **Tense**: past for facts/procedure; present for what texts say ("the statute
    provides"); past for what a prior court did, present for what its precedent stands
    for; present perfect for accumulated doctrine ("we have explained").
13. **Parallelism**: verb-form parity across (1)/(2)/(3) enumerations; conjunction before
    the final item; correlative pairs (not only/but also) placed before parallel elements.
14. **Capitalization**: this Court/the Supreme Court capped, district court lowercase;
    State capped as sovereign party, lowercase adjectivally ("state action"); defined
    short forms capped (the Act, the Board, the Rule).
15. **Deliberate fragments and And/But/So openers are licensed style** at both courts —
    the proofreader's job is to distinguish intentional fragments from broken sentences,
    not to "fix" the intentional ones.

## Variance flags (must NEVER be binary right/wrong drills)

Comma after single-word introductory adverbs; contraction use (rationed by Grant — that's/
it's/what's yes, negatives never — zero for Roberts, rare for Barrett); generic he vs. she
vs. occasional singular they; predicate-position hyphenation exceptions; "common-law"
premodifier hyphenation (inconsistent within one Grant list); University/university party
shorthand; subjunctive was/were; notional agreement with "none"; % symbol vs. "percent"
(writer-split); heading formats; spelled vs. numeral decades.

## Real errors found in published opinions (seed material for find-the-flaw passages)

"unecessary" (CASA); "together with...warn" (Horn); "third-party's" as noun possessive
(Smith & Wesson); sentence-level "?" inside quotes (Horn); *Indian/Indiana Prairie* cite
misnaming (CASA n.11); "Tr. of Oral Arg 80" missing period; "let it lay idle" (CASA);
Grant's own live slips: "neither of its claims were," "only forcible assault or
intimidation qualify."

## Curriculum weighting (drives item counts and scheduler priority)

Weight 4 (heaviest): that/which; comma splices/appositives-interrupters; quotation
punctuation; agreement across interrupters; neither-nor; institutional singulars; clear
antecedents; its/it's; list parallelism; dangling modifiers; s-ending possessives;
attributive/predicate hyphenation; Court/court; tense regime.
Weight 3: serial comma; semicolon/colon/dash mechanics; who/whom; correlative parallelism;
series punctuation; misplaced/only; plural & joint possessives; suspended hyphens; -ly;
State/state; numbers; quote fidelity; high-frequency confused pairs (affect/effect,
principal/principle, lie/lay, tortious/tortuous group).
Weight 2 and below: introductory commas (taught as tendency); date commas; indefinite
pronouns; vague this/that; closed compounds (house style); remaining confused pairs;
style-myths module (taught as myths, one item each).
